Section 01

The Business Problem

Uber's entire operation lives or dies on one question asked thousands of times a day: where will people need rides, and when? If drivers aren't already near a venue when the concert ends, riders wait ten minutes, open Lyft, and Uber loses the fare — and the customer. If too many drivers flood an area that turns quiet, they burn gas and earn nothing, and they stop showing up for the next shift. Demand forecasting is how Uber solves this. By analyzing historical trip patterns — time of day, day of week, weather, local events, university calendars — the algorithm predicts ride demand 30 to 90 minutes into the future and uses that number to decide how aggressively to recruit nearby drivers and whether to activate surge pricing. Get the forecast right and the marketplace runs smoothly: drivers earn, riders move, and Uber takes its cut. Get it wrong and the feedback loops collapse — bad forecasts produce bad experiences, which erode trust, which shrink the rider pool the next forecast is trained on. This lab builds that system from the ground up.

Section 02

The Data & the Model

I simulated two years of historical Friday night Uber trip data near WWU's campus in Bellingham — 104 weekly observations encoding a real upward growth trend, summer seasonality, a school-year effect tied to WWU's academic calendar, and a New Year's Eve spike. From that raw timeline I engineered four features the regression model could actually learn from: week number (capturing long-run growth), is_summer, is_school_year, and is_nye. I then split the data 78/26 — training the model on the first 18 months and holding the final 6 months back as a backtest window the model never saw during training.

Stress-testing isn't pessimism — it's professional discipline. A real analyst never ships a recommendation without first asking: what's the weakest assumption in this model, and what happens to the decision if that assumption is wrong? In demand forecasting, the weakest assumption is almost always the one you didn't model explicitly — here, the concert itself. The model knows it's a school-year Friday; it doesn't know 3,000 people are about to pour out of a venue at the same moment. Stress-testing forces you to name that gap before your operations manager discovers it at 10:05 PM.

Section 05

When the Algorithm Decides

The forecast number doesn't stay on a dashboard waiting for a human to review it — Uber's pricing engine consumes it automatically and sets the surge multiplier within seconds, with no analyst in the loop. When the model is right, this is elegant: drivers get a push notification, they head toward campus, and by the time the crowd hits the sidewalk there are enough cars. When the model is wrong — say, a minor earthquake shuts down a bridge the model has never seen before, rerouting thousands of people into the same pickup zone — the algorithm reads the sudden trip-request spike as "high demand" and cranks the surge multiplier to 4× or 5×, making rides unaffordable precisely when displaced people need them most. The model isn't malicious. It's doing exactly what it was trained to do. The problem is that the gap between predicting demand and deciding pricing was crossed without a human ever being asked.

Section 06

When the Model Breaks

Distribution shift detected — the world the model was trained on no longer exists. Predictions will degrade silently until the model is retrained on new data.

Six weeks after the concert, something changed in Bellingham: WWU's enrollment dropped by four percent, a new apartment complex opened in Cordata pulling students away from the walkable core near campus, and Lyft re-entered the market with a driver incentive program. The historical data the model trained on reflected none of this — it learned the old world, and it kept predicting the old world's demand. Drivers were pre-positioned for a crowd that didn't fully materialize. They earned less per shift, some stopped logging on Friday nights, and the reduced supply meant the riders who did show up waited longer or opened Lyft instead. Nobody told the drivers their schedule was based on a stale model. Nobody told the riders why wait times had crept up. The algorithm just quietly kept being wrong — a little more wrong each week — until someone noticed the MAE creeping upward in a monitoring dashboard and triggered a retrain.
