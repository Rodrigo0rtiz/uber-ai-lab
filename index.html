Section 01

The Business Problem

Uber's entire operation lives or dies on one question asked thousands of times a day: where will people need rides, and when? If drivers aren't already near a venue when the concert ends, riders wait ten minutes, open Lyft, and Uber loses the fare — and the customer. If too many drivers flood an area that turns quiet, they burn gas and earn nothing, and they stop showing up for the next shift. Demand forecasting is how Uber solves this. By analyzing historical trip patterns — time of day, day of week, weather, local events, university calendars — the algorithm predicts ride demand 30 to 90 minutes into the future and uses that number to decide how aggressively to recruit nearby drivers and whether to activate surge pricing. Get the forecast right and the marketplace runs smoothly: drivers earn, riders move, and Uber takes its cut. Get it wrong and the feedback loops collapse — bad forecasts produce bad experiences, which erode trust, which shrink the rider pool the next forecast is trained on. This lab builds that system from the ground up.

Section 02

The Data & the Model

I simulated two years of historical Friday night Uber trip data near WWU's campus in Bellingham — 104 weekly observations encoding a real upward growth trend, summer seasonality, a school-year effect tied to WWU's academic calendar, and a New Year's Eve spike. From that raw timeline I engineered four features the regression model could actually learn from: week number (capturing long-run growth), is_summer, is_school_year, and is_nye. I then split the data 78/26 — training the model on the first 18 months and holding the final 6 months back as a backtest window the model never saw during training.

Model coefficients — what linear regression learned
Feature Coefficient What it means
week_num +1.17 Each passing week adds ~1 trip as the market grows
is_summer +4.17 Summer Fridays add ~4 trips (smaller than expected — school effect dominates)
is_nye +217.38 NYE adds 217 trips — by far the single strongest signal
is_school_year −4.17 Negative because it's collinear with is_summer — the two features share the seasonal signal
intercept +317.71 Baseline trips at week 0 with all flags off

The backtest ran on weeks 79–104 — six months the model never saw during training. Across that window, actual demand ranged from 261 trips (late December winter break) to 495 trips (a strong August Friday). The model tracked the broad trend well in July and early August, but showed its largest gaps in weeks where noise and event effects pulled actual demand away from the smooth trend line — particularly the 76-trip miss on August 2nd and the 73-trip miss on July 12th. Those are weeks where real-world randomness exceeded the ±15% band the model expected.

Solid line = what actually happened.   Dashed line = what the model predicted.   The gap between them is the model's error — and the honest measure of how much to trust it.

Section 04

Stress-Testing the Forecast

Before sending that memo, I stress-tested the key assumptions to understand how fragile the recommendation was.

Step 4 simulator — WWU Concert Forecast stress-test

The lab's Step 4 simulator loaded the concert forecast with the same three assumptions from the Step 3 memo — 25 drivers online, 3.0 trips per driver per hour, and a base demand of 850 trips. At baseline, driver capacity was only 75 trips (25 × 3.0), leaving a shortfall of 775 trips and 284 drivers needed — with SURGE: ON from the very first setting. Each slider changed one assumption at a time while holding the others constant. The surge indicator never turned off across any combination tested.

Assumption tested Baseline Driver capacity Trips shortfall Surge?
Base (25 drivers × 3 trips/hr) 850 trips 75 trips 775 ON ⚡
Drivers online → 60 (max) 850 trips 300 trips 550 ON ⚡
Trips/hr → 5.0 (fast conditions) 850 trips 125 trips 725 ON ⚡
Demand → Low −15% scenario 723 trips 75 trips 648 ON ⚡
Trips/hr → 1.5 (heavy traffic) 850 trips 38 trips 812 ON ⚡ (worse)

Key finding: Surge never turned off — not at max drivers (60), not at fastest trip rate (5/hr), not at the low demand scenario. The supply-demand gap is so large that no realistic adjustment to the slider assumptions closes it. The assumption with the biggest practical effect was trips per driver per hour — pushing it down to 1.5 (post-concert traffic) made the shortfall worse, not better. Driver availability is the real constraint, and surge pricing is the only mechanism that addresses it.

Stress-testing is how you earn the right to send the memo. A real analyst changes one assumption at a time — driver count, trip efficiency, demand level — and checks whether the recommendation holds. When the conclusion survives every scenario you can construct, you have not just a forecast but a robust forecast: one your operations manager can act on even if conditions on Friday night turn out different than expected.

Section 05

When the Algorithm Decides

The forecast number doesn't stay on a dashboard waiting for a human to review it — Uber's pricing engine consumes it automatically and sets the surge multiplier within seconds, with no analyst in the loop. When the model is right, this is elegant: drivers get a push notification, they head toward campus, and by the time the crowd hits the sidewalk there are enough cars. When the model is wrong — say, a minor earthquake shuts down a bridge the model has never seen before, rerouting thousands of people into the same pickup zone — the algorithm reads the sudden trip-request spike as "high demand" and cranks the surge multiplier to 4× or 5×, making rides unaffordable precisely when displaced people need them most. The model isn't malicious. It's doing exactly what it was trained to do. The problem is that the gap between predicting demand and deciding pricing was crossed without a human ever being asked.

Section 06

When the Model Breaks

Distribution shift detected — the world the model was trained on no longer exists. Predictions will degrade silently until the model is retrained on new data.

Six weeks after the concert, something changed in Bellingham: WWU's enrollment dropped by twelve percent, a new apartment complex opened on the north side pulling students away from the walkable core near campus, and Lyft quietly launched in Whatcom County, pulling about fifteen percent of regular drivers to their platform. The historical data the model trained on reflected none of this — it learned the old world, and it kept predicting the old world's demand. Drivers were pre-positioned for a crowd that didn't fully materialize. They earned less per shift, some stopped logging on Friday nights, and the reduced supply meant riders who did show up waited longer or opened Lyft instead. Nobody told the drivers their schedule was based on a stale model. Nobody told the riders why wait times had crept up.

The alert didn't fire until week 95 — eleven weeks after the world changed at week 84. For those eleven weeks, every automated decision downstream was based on a world that no longer existed. The model wasn't broken. It was just wrong, invisibly, until someone noticed the MAE climbing in a monitoring dashboard and triggered a retrain. That eleven-week gap is the real cost of deploying a model without governance.

Section 07 — Step 8 Reflection

My Analysis

You have now seen Uber's AI system from every angle — analyst, builder, stress-tester, observer of the surge engine, and driver navigating distribution shift. The prompt below is the exact Step 8 question from the lab. The answer is written in my own words.

Step 8 prompt — from the lab

"You have now seen Uber's AI system from every angle — analyst, builder, stress-tester, observer, and driver. In one paragraph, describe how the steps connect: how a decision made at the data stage in Step 1 ultimately affects what a rider pays and what a driver earns six weeks later. Then add one sentence: if you were building this forecasting system for a real Bellingham business, what is the one thing you would add that this model didn't have?"

When it came to building the model, it first started with introducing data and signals like trends, seasonality, school effects, and NYE spikes. That way, the model has something to learn from and build patterns. However, the model was not perfect, and in this lab we went through the scenario of a concert without introducing concert variables to the model. These changes had effects in the next steps, showing how each step is interconnected. By the time we reach the end, where it comes to driver pay and customer wait times — two statistics that determine whether Uber is performing well — if the model is underpaying drivers or is not transparent enough, drivers leave. Fewer drivers means more wait time for customers, which leads to frustration and customers potentially switching to other services. This loop would continue to grow and, if not addressed, would result in reputation and profit loss. Six weeks after the concert, when WWU enrollment dropped and the model had no idea, every wrong prediction it made was a direct result of the data decisions made in Step 1, causing the model to not be able to detect the shift because an enrollment variable was never shown to the model.

If I were to remake this model, I would incorporate a variety of data. For example, we only looked at WWU, but we also have BTC and Whatcom Community College. I would incorporate that data. I would also look at other areas, not just downtown. An area like near the airport may not seem as important, but there may be hidden patterns that influence downtown demand. For example, if downtown sees high demand on a Tuesday, incorporating airport data might show that five planes land that day compared to two or three on others. Learning that it's not just about what the numbers say and even if the data is correct, the actual context and meaning to it is still very much important. Like we saw when the Uber model was surge pricing during crisis events, the model was not wrong. It was simply not trained and not enough AI governance was introduced. Leading to the backlash that Uber faced afterwards. While also still being transparent in the system, you provide that has the ultimate say in your drivers' salary is very much what separates a company from being an entity to an actual workplace that someone would want to consider.
